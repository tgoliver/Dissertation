\begin{chapterabstract}
Topography at the core mantle boundary (CMB) allows for angular momentum exchange between the core and mantle and likely generates observable signatures in the geomagnetic field.
The effects of CMB topography on core convection and the corresponding torques are studied with direct numerical simulations of rotating spherical shell convection with finite amplitude boundary topography. 
A range of topographic shapes are used, including a model that represents seismically inferred topography at the CMB and simplified shapes represented in terms of single spherical harmonics. As predicted by Bell and Soward (1996)\cite{pB96}, a new instability arises with topography for Rayleigh numbers below the onset of convection; we find reasonable qualitative agreement with their theory. The shape of the geostrophic contours, or lines of constant axial height, play an important role in determining when topography has a significant influence on fluid flows and heat transport. A high degree of equatorial symmetry leads to buoyantly-driven geostrophic flows which then feed back on to the convection. These effects can be profound for relatively small topographic amplitude and likely play a role in the core. Frequency-domain analysis shows that the small-scale convection is the main contributor to topographic torques across the range of topography shapes investigated. Previous work found that the magnitude of topographic torques scales linearly with topographic amplitude and quadratically with flow speeds. We confirm this relationship and provide new theory to estimate topographic torques for global scale, spectrally broad topography.
\end{chapterabstract}
